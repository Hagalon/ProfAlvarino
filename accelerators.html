Linear Accelerators

A linear accelerator (LINAC) is a type of particle accelerator that accelerates charged particles along a straight path using alternating electric fields. The particles gain energy as they travel through a series of cylindrical electrodes of increasing length.

Key features: Straight-line acceleration, can accelerate both electrons and protons, used in radiation therapy and research.

Betatron

A betatron is a circular accelerator that uses electromagnetic induction to accelerate electrons in a circular path. It works by increasing the magnetic field strength as the electrons gain energy, keeping them in a constant orbit.

Key features: Circular design, accelerates only electrons, uses changing magnetic field for acceleration, historically important for high-energy physics.

Cyclotron

A cyclotron is a circular accelerator that uses a constant magnetic field and alternating electric fields to accelerate charged particles in a spiral path. Particles gain energy each time they cross the gap between two "D-shaped" electrodes.

Key features: Spiral acceleration path, constant magnetic field, used for proton therapy and isotope production, compact design compared to synchrotrons.

Synchrotron

A synchrotron is a circular particle accelerator that uses synchronized electric and magnetic fields to accelerate charged particles to extremely high energies. Unlike a cyclotron, it can accelerate particles to near-light speeds by adjusting both fields as particles gain energy.

How it works:

  • Particles are first pre-accelerated in a LINAC or cyclotron
  • They are then injected into a circular vacuum tube (storage ring)
  • Precise magnetic fields bend the particle beam around the ring
  • Radiofrequency cavities provide accelerating electric fields
  • As particles gain energy, magnetic field strength increases to maintain their orbit

Key features:

  • Can accelerate particles to much higher energies than cyclotrons
  • Produces intense synchrotron radiation (used in research)
  • Used in particle physics (e.g., Large Hadron Collider) and materials science
  • Requires precise synchronization of electric and magnetic fields
  • Large-scale facilities (often hundreds of meters in diameter)

Applications: Particle physics research, materials analysis, medical imaging, drug development, and nanotechnology.
